One: "Expresso," or What Drinks am I Even Making?

Before we get into the actual making of the coffee, you need to know a few terms. Otherwise, how else will you know the difference between an iced caramel macchiato with 3 pumps and the same thing, but as a latte?

Drip Coffee

What it is: Also called "regular coffee" or "just a coffee," drip coffee is what you're probably most familiar with. It is made with ground beans, a filter, and boiling water.

Variations or subtypes: There are four types offered at Peet's -- extra dark roast, dark roast, medium roast, and light roast. No one drinks light roast.

How you make it: To make a cup, you need to grind your beans for a paper filter, add that boiling water, and just wait for it to drip in your cup. Add a lid and you're done. Simple!

Café au lait

What it is: You're probably also a bit familiar with café au lait, though you may not call it that. Keeping with the simple theme, this drink is only drip coffee with steamed milk added to it.

Variations or subtypes: None, other than with different milks!

How you make it: Fill your drip coffee cup up with about 75% coffee. On the bar, steam your given milk, then add it in for the last 30%.

From here on, the drinks described are going to be espresso drinks. This means that they're made with espresso shots on the bar, not at the drip machine.

Espresso

What it is: Frequently mispronounced as "expresso," espresso is a strong, small amount of coffee that has been pressed (with boiling water, of course) through a very small metal filter. They are measured in what's called a "shot," which is a single serving of espresso. It's the key ingredient in almost all of the drinks below.

Variations or subtypes: None.

How you make it: At your bar, take your portafilter and lock it into place on the bean grinder. Then, once the grinder has done its thing, place it in the machine and begin dripping! You'll know you're done when you see a light brown color in your transfer pitcher.

Red eye

What it is: The best of both worlds, baby. This drink is a cup of drip coffee with shots of espresso added.

Variations or subtypes: None,. other than varying flavors.

How you make it: Add your drip coffee to your cup, but leave an inch of room. Add your espresso shots, lid, and serve!

Americano

What it is: Named for the American version of Italian coffee, an americano is more tasty than it may seem at first. It's an easy mixture of espresso and hot (not boiling!) water.

Variations or subtypes: None,. other than varying flavors.

How you make it: First, add your hot water to your cup. Leave a couple inches of room for the espresso and any cream the customer may want to add. Next, add your espresso shots. Done.

Milk steamer

What it is: Also called "steamers" or simply "steamed milk." The title is self-explanatory!

Variations or subtypes: With regular steamers, flavored syrups are added to the cup before the milk is poured. Also, with any drink made with steamed milk, the word "breve" will inevitably be thrown around. This means that, instead of using the standard milk for steaming (usually 2% milk), the customer wants you to use half-and-half.

How you make it: At the bar, fill up your pitcher with the appropriate amount of milk (we'll get to what that is soon). Next, insert the steam wand at a 45-degree angle, just below the surface of the milk, and begin steaming. You'll be done when the milk reaches 160 degrees Farenheight -- or, alternatively, when the stemer begins to make a low, rumbling sound rather than the high-pitched one it did at the beginning. Good steamed milk has the look of melted ice cream, which means the foam on top has small bubbles. This foam is an essential part of the steamed milk for every drink, so I may not mention it, but remember that it is always there. Don't forget to wipe off your wand when done!

Hot chocolate

What it is: Oh, come on. You know what hot chocolate is!

Variations or subtypes: None,. other than varying flavors.

How you make it: First, add the appropriate amount of chocolate sauce in your cup. Next, steam your milk. Add some to the cup and stir. Add the rest of your milk, whip cream, a lid, and serve!

Café Latte

What it is: Ah, the beloved latte. So many variations, so many possible combinations. We'll get to that soon -- all you need to know for now is that a latte is espresso with milk. Easy!

Variations or subtypes: None,. other than varying flavors.

How you make it: First, get your cup and add in your flavored syrup/s. Next, pull your espresso shots and add to the cup (this MUST come first!). Then, steam your milk and add it. Done!

Cappuccino

What it is: This is what I like to call the "foamy latte." It's exactly like a latte, just with more foam than usual!

Variations or subtypes: People often specify their cappuccinos to be "wet" or "dry." "Wet" means that the cappuccino is more on the side of a latte, with just a little more milk foam than normal. "Dry," however, means the milk added to the cup is mostly foam.Some people (psychopaths) like to get their cappuccinos "bone dry," meaning ALL the milk added is milk foam.

How you make it: First pull and add your shots to your cup. Next, steam your milk. This time around, you'll be steaming signifiantly less milk than before. To foam it enough that you can fill a cup, move your pitcher down so that the wand stays at the same distance below the milk line as the foam rises. Be careful - it boils over easily! Add the milk foam to your cup and serve. When you pick up a cappuccino, it should be noticeably lighter than any other drink.

Mocha latte

What it is: Easy -- just a latte with chocolate syrup, or what I like to call "a latte, but chocolate." It is a combination of hot chocolate and a latte in that it is a combination of espresso and steamed milk, and a combination of chocolate sauce and steamed milk!

Variations or subtypes: White chocolate mocha.

How you make it: First, add your chocolate sauce to your cup. Next, pull your shots and add to the cup. Stir. After that, steam and add your milk, then add whip cream and some chocolate drizzle. Done!

Latte macchiato

What it is: You've probably heard of the famed caramel macchiato, which we will go into detail on later; this is its granddaddy. I like to call it "the reverse latte."

Variations or subtypes: There exists the espresso macchiato, which is made in an espresso cup. To make it, add 1 or 2 shots to your cup, then top with foam from your steamed milk.

How you make it: First, steam your milk and add to your cup. Next, pull your espresso shots and add those to the milk, careful to keep it in one spot. The macchiato is named for the phenomenon of the steamed milk being "marked" by the espresso -- keep that in mind!

Con panna

What it is: A sweet treat is what it is. Whipped cream and espresso!

Variations or subtypes: None.

How you make it: First, pull 1 ot 2 shots and add to an espresso cup. Next, add a cute dollop of whipped cream. Done-zo.

Frappé

What it is: Another oft-ordered coffee drink. The ultimate blended, iced, frozen, caffeinated delight.

Variations or subtypes: None,. other than varying flavors.

How you make it: Hoo boy. First, pull out a blender. Add milk, blended base, your flavoring, and your coffee (sometimes this is espresso, oftentimes at Peet's this is cold brew concentrate). Add ice. Blend (on #2 at Peet's) and serve with whipped cream on top!

Two: "Variety is the Spice of Life," or Secondary Terms

That was a lot, huh? Drinks are so complicated and yet so simple. If you didn't notice already, let me give you a tip: almost all drinks are a variation of the latte, particularly the most common ones like the mocha, macchiato, and cappuccino. Now that that's out of the way, I'm going to bombard you with a few more terms that illustrate the variety at Peet's. These ones coming up are your support system; the jewelry to your outfit; the horeshoes to your hooves; secondary, and yet oh, so necessary.

Sauces

Sauces are thick, flavored liquids used to define a drink. Unlike syrups, they can completely transform a drink -- this is why the mocha latte is not just called a "latte with [x] pumps of chocolate." They're a bit more complicated to make in-house, and they're stronger and much thicker than syrups. Later, you will find that they're applied to drinks differently too!

At Peet's, you will enounter these sauces:

  • Chocolate
  • White chocolate
  • Caramel
  • Strawberry
  • Yuzu citrus

Syrups

Monin-brand syrups are probably the most frequently used additions at Peet's. Like sauces, they flavor a drink, but unlike sauces, they won't overtake it. A syrup is more of a light note, a suggestion even, of difference in your cup. The more frequently used ones in certain drinks may warrant separate drink names, like with the vanilla latte, but it's all the same formula. Unlike sauces, some syrups can be made from scratch in a pinch and some, like simple syrup, are made in-house all the time!

At Peet's, you will encounter these syrups:

  • Simple
  • Vanilla
  • Sugar-free vanilla
  • Chicory
  • Peppermint
  • Pumpkin Pie
  • Cinnamon Bun
  • Maple Pancake
  • Roasted Hazelnut
  • Coconut
  • Honey

There also are a few rotating seasonal syrups you may encounter, but the ones above are what you'll regularly work with.

Powders and Dusts

Oft-overlooked, rarely used. Powders do have a place, as you'll see once we get to recipes!

At Peet's, you will encounter these powders and dusts:

  • Matcha
  • Chocolate
  • Cinnamon
  • Cinnamon sugar

Milks

Ahh, milk. You thought at least this would be simple, huh? Nope! Unfortunately for you, Peet's offers a wide array of dairy-full and dairy-free milks!

At Peet's, you will encounter these milks:

  • Whole milk
  • 2% milk
  • Skim, or non-fat, milk
  • Half-and-half
  • Heavy cream
  • Almond milk
  • Oat milk
  • Soy milk

Teas

With all this coffee talk, I almost forgot about the teas! You will work a lot with teas, but we'll learn more about their specifics when we get to in-house recipes. The teas below aren't every available tea, but they're the ones you should keep in mind for now.

At Peet's, you will encounter these teas:

  • Green tea
  • Black tea
  • Hibiscus tea

Four: "Yes, Chef!" or, Making Ingredients In-House

Okay, now we're getting to the part where you make things! Don't get too excited, though. Making drinks is in the next section, but I'm sure this one will be just as enjoyable.

Though most of the ingredients you'll be using at Peet's comes straight to your store's door, there are a few that are made in-house. Don't worry! These items are quite simple to make, and they help to fill out your day while business is slow.

Chocolate sauce

Intro: Ah, the mighty, most-often-used-out-of-all-the-sauces chocolate sauce! This one is probably the easiest to make.

How to make it: First, you pull out two bags of chocolate powder - two batches is what fills up the chocolate sauce bucket. Then, grab 16oz (or one hot medium cup full) of boiling water. Pour them both into a blender - water first - and blend on #3. This will run for 60 seconds. Blend it one more time and you're done with one batch! Do that for the second batch and you're done x2. Make sure to label and date your chocolate sauce when it's all made and contained.

Cold brew + cold brew concentrate

Intro: Our friend Cold Brew has only been mentioned in passing so far, but now we get to meet it properly. Cold brew is what many just call "iced coffee," and making the concentrate is the first step towards that icy caffeinated goodness.

How to make it: To make it, you're going to need a few things. First, pull out a large bucket, a reusable filter, a large, square paper filter, a 3-pound bag of ICB coffee (Baridi Blend), a whisk, and a length of string. In your bucket, wrap the elastic of your reusable filter around the edges of the bucket. Then, go over to your coffee grinder and throw in those ICB beans to grind for French Press (this is usually the most coarse setting on the grinder). Grind that into your square paper filter, and place it inside that reusable filter and bucket. Next, you need to acquire 9 liters of water. Pour that water slowly in your bucket to saturate the coffee grounds, whisking as you go. Once you've got all the water in the bucket, whisk until the mixture looks smooth. Be careful, though -- whisking too vigorously can rip the paper filter! With your mixture all smooth, you can now take out the whisk and pick up your length of string. Grab the edges of your filter, twist them, and use that string to keep the edges tied together. Place a lid on your bucket and store it out of the way for 24 hours.
The next day, you have cold brew concentrate! This is used undiluted in frappés and many iced drinks. Don't use it as regular cold brew, though! This mixture is very strong. It tastes funny and can cause some...digestive issues...for those who drink too much. To make your concentrate into verifiable cold brew, mix it with 1 part water, 1 part CBC.

Tea

Intro: Tea is another ingredient we haven't spent much time on. At Peet's we serve both iced and hot, both bagged and looseleaf teas, most of which fall into the categories of black tea, green tea, and herbal tea (which is decaffinated). Here, we'll be making iced tea.

How to make it: To make your iced tea, you're going to want to pull out a 4 liter pitcher, a handheld strainer, a kettle, a large milk foam spoon, the plastic bag for your given tea, and a large ice scoop. First, pour your tea leaves into your kettle. If you're making black or hibiscus tea (hibiscus being our iced herbal option), fill the kettle with boiling water. If you're making green tea, fill it with regular hot water from the drip machine.1 Let that brew for three minutes. While you're waiting, you should add about two large scoops of ice into your pitcher and affix the handheld strainer on top. When your tea is finished, pour it, leaves and all, onto the strainer and press a little to get that last bit of tea out. Take the strainer and throw the leaves away (no leaves in the sink, please!). To your pitcher, add water until it fills up completely. Do you see that foam? Take your spoon and scrape it off into the sink. And you're done! Don't forget to cover your pitcher with saran wrap, or to label and date it.

Note 1: This is to prevent the lighter green tea leaves from burning. In my experience, though, this makes no discernible difference to most, if not all, customers.So, I usually opt for boiling water no matter the tea type.

Simple syrup

Intro: I wasn't lying when I said syrups could be made in-house. In fact, I was foreshadowing. Simple syrup is the most (say it with me) simple syrup to make!

How to make it: First, get out a whisk and a metal pitcher; the one that's a bit larger and rounder than the ones for milk steaming. Next, add 1 part boiling water and 1 part white granulated sugar to the pitcher. Whisk until everything's mixed and pour into your simple syrup container. EASY. Though simple is the only syrup made in-house, I've also seen vanilla syrup made in a pinch. To do that, follow the same instructions, but add a few teaspoons of vanilla extract.

Whipped cream

Intro: Yes, even whipped cream is made in-house!

How to make it: First, grab your freshly-made simple syrup, some heavy cream, a whip canister and lid (make sure the O-ring is in place!), and a charger. Next, put 6p simple syrup in your canister and fill it to the line with heavy cream. Screw on the lid and turn it upside down -- you may want to do this next part over a sink. Place the charger in its holder and screw that on. Hear that? That's the sound of the CO2 fluffing and mixing up the cream and syrup! Shake your canister for 30 seconds, and you're done. Don't forget to put on the day sticker before you put it in the fridge!

Now you can say that you're a bonafide chef! You can make more than just little ol' drinks. You've also just passed all of the not-so-fun parts. Yep, that's right. Next up...DRINK-MAKING!
